# Project Context: The Information Exchange

## Executive Summary

The Information Exchange is a neutral marketplace where buyers—human or AI agent—post what they need, sellers with verified expertise or ground-level access describe what they know, and the exchange brokers the match without revealing the goods before purchase. **The trust layer is the product. The outcome database is the moat. The intelligence layer is the differentiator.**

The product is three layers:
- **Base layer:** A raw information market with bid/ask mechanics.
- **Trust engine:** Verifies sellers, cross-references claims, and builds longitudinal accuracy scores from transaction outcomes.
- **Intelligence layer:** LLM-powered — decomposes complex buyer needs into atomic fulfillable requests, matches them against supply, and manages ongoing freshness. The interface to all three layers is a conversation.

> Key insight: prior data marketplaces failed because they were supply-side catalogs. This marketplace is demand-driven, with private intermediation — the exchange sits between buyer and seller, knows both sides, and brokers the match.

## Core Thesis

- **The problem:** AI agents know what was true at training time; ground truth is held by people with no reliable way to sell it. The market does not exist. Prior alternatives (Bloomberg, Premise Data, Ocean Protocol) each failed to combine demand-driven price discovery, a trust layer, and an LLM-powered intelligence layer.
- **Why now:** AI agents are real buyers (Claude, Gemini, vertical agents), protocol standards (MCP, A2A) establish the interface, LLMs enable the intelligence layer, and micropayment infrastructure is mature.
- **The moat:** The outcome database — longitudinal ground truth on seller reliability that no entrant can purchase. Combined with the verified seller network and trust brand, this compounds with every transaction.
- **The structural defense:** Discovery alone (which platforms can copy) is not the product. Discovery without trust is Google Dataset Search. Platform operators have structural disincentives to act as trusted intermediaries. The trust layer cannot be purchased.

## Strategic Priorities

1. Run an open bounty board (Weeks 1–8) as a market research instrument before committing to a vertical.
2. Lead with a healthcare compliance product (provider directory certification) to reach $1M ARR faster — use the exchange to fulfill it, use the revenue to subsidize exchange growth.
3. Accumulate trust data density within 18–24 months; first-mover advantage is in trust data accumulation, not the idea.

## Key Risks to Keep in Mind

- Adverse selection in early supply (budget heavily for manual QC in Year 1)
- Enterprise sales timing (founder runs first conversations personally)
- Platform incumbent entry (mitigated by neutrality advantage and trust layer depth)
